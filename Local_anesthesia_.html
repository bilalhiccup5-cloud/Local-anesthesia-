Definition & Key Characteristics üîë +

General Anesthesia (GA) is a **reversible depressant of the CNS** used for surgical operations without pain or unpleasant side effects.

GA is Characterized By:

  • Unconsciousness
  • Analgesia (Pain relief)
  • Amnesia (Memory loss)
  • Skeletal muscle relaxation
  • Loss of reflexes
Stages of General Anesthesia üö¶ +
1. Stage of Analgesia:
  • Starts at administration, lasts until loss of consciousness.
  • Patient feels a **dream-like state**.
  • Reflexes and respiration remain normal.
2. Stage of Delirium (Excitement):
  • From loss of consciousness to onset of irregular respiration.
  • **Apparent excitement**, increased muscle tone, tightly closed jaws.
  • Heart rate and BP may rise.
3. Stage of Surgical Anesthesia (4 Planes):
  • From onset of irregular respiration to cessation of spontaneous breathing.
  • Plane 1: Eyes become fixed.
  • Plane 2: Loss of corneal and laryngeal reflexes.
  • Plane 3: Pupil starts dilating, loss of light reflex.
  • Plane 4: Dilated pupil, decreased muscle tone, BP falls.
4. Stage of Medullary Paralysis:
  • Respiratory and vasomotor control ceases, leading to **Death**.
Potency & Pharmacokinetic Principles üß™ +

Minimum Alveolar Concentration (MAC)

The concentration at which 50% of subjects have no movement response to a surgical stimulus (skin incision). It's different for each inhaled agent.

Governing Characteristics of Inhalational Anesthetics

  • Oil:Gas Partition Coefficient (Fat Solubility): A measure of anesthetic **POTENCY**. Higher solubility $\rightarrow$ greater anesthetic action.
  • Blood:Gas Partition Coefficient (Blood Solubility): Lower B/G coefficient $\rightarrow$ **rapid induction & recovery**.

Pre-Anaesthetic Medication üíä +

Drugs used **before** anesthesia to make it pleasant, safe, and prevent complications.

  • To relieve anxiety: Benzodiazepines
  • To provide analgesia and amnesia: Morphine
  • To control secretions/prevent aspiration: Atropine
  • To prevent P.O. Nausea/Vomiting: Antiemetics
  • To control infection: Antibiotics
  • To induce sedation/reduce metabolic rate: Sedatives
  • To provide hemodynamic stability: I/V fluids
Halothane: Merits & Demerits ‚ö†Ô∏è +

Merits (+)

  • Potent anesthetic agent.
  • No stage of excitement.
  • Not irritant to respiratory passage; good for children and asthmatics.
  • Produces hypotension, reducing blood loss.
  • Potentiates muscle relaxation (e.g., by tubocurarine).

Demerits (-)

  • Very low margin of safety.
  • Sensitizes myocardium to adrenaline (causing dysrhythmias).
  • Causes hypotension and bradycardia; depresses respiration.
  • Poor analgesic (co-administered with Nitrous Oxide).
  • Contraindicated in labor (decreases uterine contractions).
  • Can cause **malignant hyperthermia** & **Hepatitis**.
Nitrous Oxide: Merits & Demerits ‚òÅÔ∏è +

Merits (+)

  • Non-irritant to respiratory tract.
  • Smooth and rapid induction and recovery.
  • Powerful analgesic (used in labor/burns dressing).
  • Least hepatotoxic and least cardiac depressant.
  • Does not cause respiratory depression.
  • Used as **adjuvant** to more potent anesthetics.

Demerits (-)

  • Not a potent general anesthetic (needs combination).
  • Inadequate muscle relaxation.
  • Moves rapidly, causing **air pockets to expand** (e.g., in abdomen, lung, or pleura $\rightarrow$ pneumothorax).
